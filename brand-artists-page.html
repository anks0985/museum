Japanese Masters

Explore the extraordinary legacy of Japan's ukiyo-e movement through our curated collection of prints and paintings by the most influential artists of the Edo period, whose works continue to inspire and captivate audiences worldwide.

About the Collection

The Japanese Masters collection represents the pinnacle of ukiyo-e art from the Edo period (1603-1868), a golden age of Japanese printmaking. Ukiyo-e, which translates to "pictures of the floating world," depicted the transient pleasures and beauty of everyday life in Japan.

These master artists revolutionized woodblock printing techniques, creating works characterized by bold compositions, vibrant colors, and meticulous attention to detail. Their subjects ranged from serene landscapes and dramatic natural phenomena to intimate portraits and scenes from literature and theater.

When Japan opened to Western trade in the 1850s, these prints flowed into Europe, profoundly influencing Impressionist and Post-Impressionist movements. The flattened perspective, asymmetrical compositions, and emphasis on natural forms found in ukiyo-e became integral to the development of modern Western art.

Featured Artists

Katsushika Hokusai

1760-1849

The creator of the iconic "Great Wave off Kanagawa," Hokusai was a prolific artist who produced an estimated 30,000 works during his lifetime. His series "Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji" revolutionized landscape art and had a profound influence on Western Impressionism.

Explore Artist

Utagawa Hiroshige

1797-1858

Known as the "Artist of Rain, Snow, and Mist," Hiroshige was a master of atmospheric landscape prints. His series "Fifty-three Stations of the Tōkaidō" and "One Hundred Famous Views of Edo" showcase his extraordinary ability to capture seasonal changes and weather conditions.

Explore Artist

Kitagawa Utamaro

1753-1806

Celebrated for his exquisite portraits of women, Utamaro perfected the bijin-ga (beautiful women) genre. His intimate and psychologically nuanced depictions of courtesans and geisha reveal both their public personas and private emotions, often using innovative close-up compositions.
