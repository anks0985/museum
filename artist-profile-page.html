Katsushika Hokusai

A towering figure in Japanese art, Hokusai revolutionized ukiyo-e woodblock printing and influenced Western Impressionism with his iconic landscapes and dramatic compositions.

Biography

Katsushika Hokusai (1760-1849) was a Japanese artist, ukiyo-e painter and printmaker of the Edo period. Born in Edo (now Tokyo), he is best known as author of the woodblock print series "Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji" which includes the iconic print "The Great Wave off Kanagawa."

From humble beginnings as the son of an artisan-mirror maker, Hokusai began painting at age six and was apprenticed to a woodcarver at 14. His career spanned seven decades, during which he produced an estimated 30,000 works and adopted at least 30 different names to mark significant periods in his artistic development.

Hokusai's style is characterized by its striking compositions, vibrant use of color, and dynamic portrayal of subjects, whether landscapes, people, or mythical beasts. His innovation in perspective and compositional techniques revolutionized ukiyo-e printmaking and heavily influenced European Impressionists like Monet, Van Gogh, and Degas after Japanese isolationism ended in the 1850s.
